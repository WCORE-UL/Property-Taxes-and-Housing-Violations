# Eda Analysis

## Violations vs Tax Payment
The dot plot indicates that there is a slight positive relationship between tax payment and violation count. This, I would interpret, is a 
result of apartments(which pay higher property tax per building) also would have the greatest number of housing violations thanks to their 
large number of people per building.  

## Violations by Tax Class
A tax class refers to a specific rate of taxation that a type of building in an area must pay. In the case of this graph, The classes which incur the most violations
are those in urban and municipal areas. This reinforces the theory that the areas which have the most violations are high density, likely apartments or condos

## Type of violation by Tax Class
This graph displays the tax classes with the most violations, broken down by the types of violations that are most prevalent.
It shows that the most common form of violation, across the tax spectrum, are those pertaining to walls/ceilings, doors, and solid waste.
R038, the largest of the classes, refers to the southeastern part of montgomery county around Rockville and the Bethesda outskirts. 
R042, the second largest, refers to the greater silver spring area. 

## Average Tax Payment
This map is particularly skewed by the presence of 2 zip codes. These codes refer to the north bethesda area. These areas have an inflated
average tax payment as a result of having very few addresses recorded. The map includes a filter to see the difference between the two.
For the most useful patterns, we will be using the filtered version. The map indicates that the codes that pay the highest average tax rates
are centered around the areas near DC. This is to be expected as areas with lower commute times generally have higher rates. 

## Average number of violations
This map indicates that bethesda has a significantly greater average than most other regions. It is much more polarizing than the prior map
and indicates that there is likely something more than tax payment that is boosting violation rates. 

## Average Elapsed Time
This map shows average time taken between a violations filing and its resolution. Interestingly this map is much more spread out than its predeccesors.
The more distant, less urban areas have the longest time, but not by a significant margin. There does not appear to be a strong correlation between the number of violations
and the time taken to resolve them. 

## Map of Tax Class
This dotplot displays where the tax classes cover. It highlights how these classes are specific to certain areas and are not just flat rates. It also shows how 
the space the rates cover varies signficantly, with R042(Silver Spring) covering almost half of MOCO.
